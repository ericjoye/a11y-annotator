How to Audit Your Website for Accessibility in 5 Minutes

July 08, 2026 · 4 min read

Most frontend developers know they should check accessibility. Most don't — not because they don't care, but because the tooling friction is too high. Here's a 5-minute workflow that catches the most common issues.

Step 1: Install a Local Checker

The biggest mistake is relying on online scanners. They test one URL at a time, miss dynamic content, and can't evaluate interactions that require user input. You need something that runs in the browser you're developing in.

a11y-annotator ($4.99 one-time) installs as a Chrome extension and works on any page you're viewing — localhost, staging, or production.

Step 2: Run a Scan

Open the extension popup and click 'Scan page.' In seconds you'll get a report showing:

  • Which WCAG criteria failed and which passed
  • Exact elements that need fixing
  • Severity ratings so you can triage

Step 3: Annotate and Export

Use the annotation pins to mark issues directly on the page. This is much easier than sending screenshots — your developer or designer can click the pin and see exactly what needs fixing.

Export the report as Markdown and paste it into your issue tracker or pull request. Now accessibility issues are tracked the same way as any other bug.

Why Automated Checking Matters

Manual accessibility testing is essential — automated tools can only catch about 30% of potential issues. But that 30% is the low-hanging fruit that you can fix in minutes. Missing alt text, low-contrast text, missing form labels — these are the things users actually notice every day.
